PhD Candidate

I am a doctoral candidate in the History of Science department at Harvard University. My research focuses on the history of medicine, public health, gender, and religion in the modern United States.

My dissertation explores the role of euthenic programming in Progressive Era urban child welfare and maternal reform. While eugenics is the subject of extensive historiography, I center my analysis on euthenics, a lesser-known movement among women that addressed the existing conditions in which people were reproducing and raising future generations by controlling the environment through hygiene, sanitation, and nutrition. I focus on the New York City Babiesâ€™ Welfare Association (BWA), a city-wide preventative care network for immigrant women and children made up of hundreds of philanthropic, religious, and city-funded organizations. In treating child welfare reformers as public health workers, my dissertation demonstrates the ways eugenic environmental reform permeated public health programs.

My research has been supported by fellowships from various institutions including the New York Academy of Medicine, the American Philosophical Society, Vassar College, the Consortium for the History of Science, Technology and Medicine, the Charles Warren Center for American Studies, and the Committee on the Study of Religion at Harvard University.

Before joining the Ph.D. program, I worked in digital marketing and public relations for a variety of industries, including healthcare and education. I hold an AM from Harvard University, an MA from the University of Chicago and a BA from the University of Portland.
